# ECAS Design Context

## Product summary
ECAS is a self-contained portfolio demo of a B2B electronics procurement platform. It is not a lifestyle storefront and not a generic SaaS dashboard. It needs to feel like a serious commercial tool where buyers can move from discovery to order review with confidence.

The repo matters as a case-study artifact because it shows three layers in one product:
- storefront discovery
- protected account / checkout flows
- dashboard and order-management surfaces

## Primary audience
### Real product users
- Procurement managers sourcing electronic components for commercial or industrial teams
- Operations coordinators checking availability, pricing, order details, and account status
- Technical buyers who need dense information, fast scanning, and a dependable path to checkout

### Portfolio audience
- Hiring managers and portfolio reviewers
- Product/design leads evaluating judgment, polish, information architecture, and end-to-end UX thinking

## Core jobs to be done
1. Find relevant parts quickly through search, category browsing, and product detail pages
2. Understand stock, availability, pricing context, and purchase options without ambiguity
3. Move cleanly from product discovery to cart and order summary
4. Enter protected flows with minimal friction and clear sign-in expectations
5. Review order/account/dashboard information without feeling lost in admin complexity
6. Demonstrate a believable MVP for portfolio review without backend dependencies

## What a reviewer should understand fast
Within the first minute, a reviewer should grasp:
- this is a B2B electronics procurement product
- the app supports more than a landing page: browse, product detail, cart, sign-in, order summary, dashboard
- the UX is structured for work, not entertainment
- the product has enough cohesion to feel MVP-ready

## Brand voice
- Precise
- Trustworthy
- Industrial
- Direct
- Calm under pressure

## Desired interface feel
The interface should feel:
- fast to scan
- competent and structured
- operationally credible
- polished without becoming luxurious or editorial-for-its-own-sake
- dense where needed, but never cluttered
- calm and reliable rather than flashy

## Theme direction
Prefer a light-first interface. This product is used in work contexts where clarity, legibility, and information density matter more than mood. If darker areas appear, they should feel controlled and functional rather than cinematic or trendy.

## Visual direction
Aim for:
- strong hierarchy
- disciplined spacing
- clear page landmarks
- restrained accent usage
- trustworthy product density
- frictionless CTA language

This should feel closer to a high-trust industrial catalog + procurement dashboard than to a startup marketing template.

## Visual references
- High-trust B2B product surfaces with crisp hierarchy and strong information architecture
- Industrial catalogs and procurement tools that feel exact, orderly, and efficient
- Product dashboards with disciplined spacing, practical typography, and restrained accent color usage
- Commerce experiences where the purchase path is obvious and confidence-building

## Anti-references
Avoid anything that makes ECAS feel like generic AI-generated SaaS or consumer hype:
- purple/blue neon gradients
- glassmorphism
- loud startup hero energy
- soft DTC lifestyle e-commerce styling
- repeated same-size card grids everywhere
- over-decorated dashboards
- fake luxury cues
- loud Black Friday promo language
- placeholder-copy energy
- decorative effects that reduce credibility

## UX priorities
1. Make the main review path obvious: storefront → product → cart / order summary → sign-in → dashboard / account
2. Remove dead ends, placeholder copy, and confusing labels
3. Keep hierarchy sharp so a reviewer can understand each screen in seconds
4. Favor functional elegance over visual theatrics
5. Preserve working flows in mock mode while improving presentation quality
6. Ensure CTAs and state changes feel intentional, not ambiguous

## Screen-specific priorities
### Storefront
- Should immediately read as electronics procurement, not generic retail
- Category, search, and product scanning must feel fast and credible
- Promo surfaces should support the product narrative, not distract from it

### Product + cart + order summary
- Need especially clear hierarchy and next-step guidance
- Important information should feel ordered, not noisy
- Checkout entry should feel safe, explicit, and low-friction

### Sign-in / account entry
- Reviewer path should be obvious
- Demo roles should not create confusion
- Copy should explain why sign-in matters without sounding defensive

### Dashboard
- Should feel useful and structured, not overwhelming
- Tables, summary cards, and account surfaces should feel like part of the same product system as the storefront

## Typography direction
Typography should support clarity and credibility first. Prefer a distinctive but disciplined system, not fashionable contrast for its own sake. Avoid the feeling of a decorative concept portfolio piece. This product should read as sharp, serious, and dependable.

## Color direction
Use color to clarify hierarchy and action, not to create spectacle. Accent colors should guide attention sparingly. Neutrals should feel slightly industrial, not sterile and not playful.

## Implementation constraints
- Nuxt 3 + Vue 3 + TypeScript + Tailwind CSS
- Existing storefront, auth, and dashboard flows must keep working
- Mock mode is the default review environment
- Prefer incremental refinement over full rewrites unless a screen is fundamentally broken
- Accessibility, scannability, and responsive behavior matter more than novelty alone
- Improvements should strengthen the case study, not destabilize the demo

## Case-study success bar
ECAS should feel MVP-ready for portfolio presentation:
- coherent
- trustworthy
- easy to demo
- visually cleaner than a raw engineering build
- clearly more thoughtful than a standard template

The goal is not to make ECAS look trendy. The goal is to make it look believable, useful, and well-judged.
